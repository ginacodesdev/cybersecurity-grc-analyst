# Working with System Owners and Administrators

![Last Reviewed](https://img.shields.io/badge/Last%20Reviewed-March%202026-green)

---

## Overview

A GRC Analyst does not work in isolation. The role
is fundamentally collaborative and the quality of
the relationships a GRC Analyst builds with system
owners, system administrators, and other IT and
security stakeholders directly impacts the
effectiveness of the entire compliance program.
Understanding how to navigate these relationships
professionally and productively is one of the most
important and least discussed aspects of GRC work.

---

## Who the GRC Analyst Works With

| Stakeholder | Role in GRC Context |
|-------------|-------------------|
| System Owner | Accountable for the overall operation and security of a system; approves risk decisions |
| System Administrator | Responsible for the technical implementation and maintenance of system controls |
| ISSO | Manages the day-to-day security posture of a specific system |
| IT Security Team | Provides technical expertise and supports control implementation |
| Legal and Compliance | Advises on regulatory requirements and organizational risk tolerance |
| Auditors | Internal or external reviewers who assess the organization's compliance posture |
| Leadership and Executives | Make risk acceptance decisions and receive program status reporting |

---

## The GRC Analyst as Coordinator

The GRC Analyst rarely implements controls directly.
That work belongs to the technical teams. The GRC
Analyst's role is to ensure that the right work is
happening, that it is documented correctly, and
that it aligns with the requirements of the applicable
frameworks and the organization's security program.

This coordination role requires the GRC Analyst to
understand enough about the technical work to ask
the right questions without overstepping into the
domain of the engineers and administrators who own
the implementation. The ability to speak both the
language of compliance and the language of technology
is what makes a GRC Analyst genuinely effective in
this space.

---

## Building Productive Working Relationships

Technical teams are often skeptical of compliance
work. They have operational responsibilities that
compete for their time and attention. Security
requirements can feel like additional burden on top
of already demanding workloads. A GRC Analyst who
approaches these relationships with a collaborative
mindset rather than a policing mindset will
consistently get better outcomes.

This means explaining why a requirement exists and
not just that it exists. It means understanding the
operational constraints that make some remediations
difficult and working with teams to find practical
paths forward. It means being a resource that helps
people navigate compliance requirements rather than
a checkpoint that creates obstacles.

---

## Communicating Compliance Requirements Clearly

One of the most valuable things a GRC Analyst can
do for the people they work with is translate
framework language into plain operational terms.
A control requirement written in regulatory or
framework language is not always immediately
actionable for a system administrator. The GRC
Analyst who can bridge that gap adds real value
to the implementation process.

Clear communication also means being specific
about what is needed and by when. Vague requests
for documentation or unclear expectations about
remediation timelines create friction and delay.
The more clearly a GRC Analyst communicates
requirements, the more efficiently the teams
they work with can respond.

---

## Navigating Resistance and Competing Priorities

Not every stakeholder will embrace security and
compliance requirements enthusiastically. Some
will push back on timelines. Others will deprioritize
remediation in favor of operational demands. A
GRC Analyst needs to be prepared to navigate
these situations professionally and persistently.

Escalation is a tool that should be used thoughtfully
and not as a first response. When a stakeholder
is consistently unable to meet remediation commitments,
the GRC Analyst should first seek to understand
the barriers, then work collaboratively to address
them, and escalate to leadership only when the
situation genuinely requires it. Escalation that
is used prematurely damages working relationships
and reduces the GRC Analyst's effectiveness over time.

---

## Documentation as a Shared Responsibility

Documentation is not only the GRC Analyst's
responsibility. System owners and administrators
contribute to implementation statements, POAM
updates, and audit evidence. The GRC Analyst's
role is to ensure that documentation standards
are clear, that the right people understand what
is expected of them, and that contributions from
technical teams are reviewed for completeness
and accuracy before they become part of the
official program record.

When documentation responsibilities are shared
clearly and consistently, the overall quality of
the program's record improves and the burden on
any single person is reduced.
