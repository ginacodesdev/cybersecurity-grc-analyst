# Core Responsibilities of a GRC Analyst

![Last Reviewed](https://img.shields.io/badge/Last%20Reviewed-March%202026-green)

---

## Overview

A GRC Analyst's role is built around three major
pillars — governance, risk management, and compliance.
Within those pillars there are specific responsibilities
that show up consistently across organizations and
industries. This page breaks down what those
responsibilities actually look like in practice,
not just how they are described in a job posting.

---

## Responsibility 1 — Managing and Implementing the Security Program

The GRC Analyst plays a central role in building,
maintaining, and continuously improving the
organization's cybersecurity program. This includes
ensuring that policies and procedures are current
and aligned with applicable frameworks, that security
controls are documented and tracked, and that the
program is maturing in response to evolving threats
and organizational changes.

Program management in GRC is not just about
documentation. It requires understanding the business
context of the systems being secured, the regulatory
environment the organization operates in, and the
risk tolerance of the leadership team. A GRC Analyst
who understands the business is significantly more
effective than one who only understands the frameworks.

---

## Responsibility 2 — Remediation Plan Management

One of the most consistent daily activities for a
GRC Analyst is managing remediation plans and POAMs.
This means reviewing open items regularly to assess
their current status, following up with system owners
and administrators to confirm that work is on track,
investigating and documenting delays when milestones
are missed, and working with responsible parties to
establish revised plans when timelines change.

Remediation management is where the GRC Analyst's
communication skills are tested most directly. Getting
technical teams to prioritize security remediation
alongside their other operational responsibilities
requires clarity, consistency, and a collaborative
approach. The GRC Analyst who builds strong working
relationships with the people responsible for
remediation will always have better outcomes than
one who relies solely on process and paperwork.

---

## Responsibility 3 — Audit Liaison

Many GRC Analysts serve as the primary point of
contact between the organization and its auditors.
This role involves coordinating audit schedules
and logistics, connecting auditors with the right
subject matter experts, collecting and organizing
the evidence artifacts that auditors request, and
tracking the status of audit findings through to
resolution.

Being an effective audit liaison requires preparation
long before the audit begins. An organization that
maintains audit-ready documentation throughout the
year will move through an audit far more efficiently
than one that scrambles to collect evidence after
the auditors arrive. The GRC Analyst is responsible
for building and maintaining that state of readiness.

---

## Responsibility 4 — ISSO Support and Documentation

In many organizations, particularly those operating
under the RMF, GRC Analysts also support or serve
as Information System Security Officers (ISSOs)
for specific systems. This involves maintaining
system-specific security documentation, coordinating
vulnerability management activities, supporting
the authorization process, and ensuring that the
system remains compliant with its approved security
posture between authorization cycles.

When a GRC Analyst serves as both a program-level
compliance resource and a system-level ISSO, they
experience the work from both perspectives simultaneously.
This dual role creates a feedback loop that deepens
understanding of how program-level requirements
translate into system-level practice.

---

## Responsibility 5 — GRC Tool Administration

GRC tools are the platforms that organizations use
to track controls, manage evidence, monitor compliance
status, and report on risk posture. Many GRC Analysts
serve as administrators for these platforms within
their organizations, which means they are responsible
for configuring workflows, managing user access,
ensuring data accuracy, and supporting other team
members and stakeholders who use the tool.

Familiarity with GRC platforms is one of the most
practical skills a GRC Analyst can develop. Common
platforms include Archer, ServiceNow GRC, Drata,
Vanta, and others. Each platform has its own
approach but the underlying concepts of control
tracking, evidence management, and risk reporting
are consistent across all of them.

---

## The Reality of the Role

GRC work is demanding in ways that are not always
visible from the outside. It requires managing
large volumes of documentation while staying
current on evolving threats and regulatory changes.
It requires building consensus among stakeholders
who have competing priorities. It requires translating
complex technical and regulatory requirements into
language that non-technical leaders can understand
and act on.

It also requires judgment. Not every situation has
a clear answer in a framework document. The GRC
Analyst who develops sound professional judgment
alongside technical knowledge is the one who adds
the most value to the organizations they serve.
