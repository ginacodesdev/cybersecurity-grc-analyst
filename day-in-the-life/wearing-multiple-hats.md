# Wearing Multiple Hats in GRC

![Last Reviewed](https://img.shields.io/badge/Last%20Reviewed-March%202026-green)

---

## Overview

In many organizations, particularly smaller agencies,
startups, and lean security teams, a GRC Analyst
does not operate in a single defined lane. The role
frequently expands to include responsibilities that
would be separate positions in a larger organization.
Understanding this reality before entering the field
helps set accurate expectations and prepares
practitioners to navigate the complexity that
comes with wearing multiple hats.

---

## The ISSO and GRC Analyst Overlap

One of the most common dual responsibilities in
GRC work is serving simultaneously as a program-level
GRC Analyst and as an Information System Security
Officer (ISSO) for one or more specific systems.
These roles have different focuses but significant
overlap in practice.

As a program-level GRC Analyst the focus is on
the overall compliance program — policies, frameworks,
audit support, and cross-system risk visibility.
As an ISSO the focus narrows to a specific system —
its documentation, its controls, its vulnerabilities,
and its authorization status.

When a single person holds both roles they experience
an interesting dynamic. The program-level guidance
they provide to other system teams is the same
guidance they must follow for their own system.
This creates a built-in accountability loop but
also a significant workload that requires careful
time management and prioritization.

---

## Audit Liaison as an Additional Responsibility

Many GRC Analysts also serve as the organization's
audit liaison, managing the relationship between
the internal security program and external or
internal auditors. This responsibility involves
coordinating audit logistics, connecting auditors
with subject matter experts, collecting evidence,
and tracking findings through remediation.

Audit liaison work is time-intensive and cyclical.
During audit periods it can become the dominant
activity on a GRC Analyst's schedule. Outside of
audit cycles the focus shifts back to program
maintenance and remediation management. A GRC
Analyst who serves as audit liaison needs to
be organized, responsive, and comfortable
managing relationships with multiple stakeholders
simultaneously.

---

## GRC Tool Administrator as a Third Role

As covered in the GRC tool administration section,
many GRC Analysts also serve as the administrator
for the organization's compliance platform. Questions
about access, configuration, and how to use the
tool tend to flow to the person who knows it best
which is often the GRC Analyst who manages it daily.

This administrative responsibility adds a support
dimension to the role that is not always visible
in a job description. It requires availability
for ad hoc questions and troubleshooting alongside
the primary compliance work.

---

## Managing the Workload

Working across multiple roles simultaneously
requires deliberate time management and the
ability to shift context quickly. A few practices
that help GRC professionals manage this kind of
workload effectively include blocking dedicated
time for deep documentation work rather than
trying to fit it between interruptions, batching
similar tasks such as POAM reviews or stakeholder
follow-ups into focused sessions, and communicating
clearly with leadership about workload when
competing demands cannot all be met at the same
time.

It is also important to document work consistently
regardless of how busy the schedule becomes.
Documentation gaps created during high-demand
periods are often the ones that surface as audit
findings later.

---

## The Value of Breadth

While wearing multiple hats creates real workload
challenges, it also builds a depth of experience
that is difficult to acquire in a more specialized
role. A GRC Analyst who has served as an ISSO,
an audit liaison, and a tool administrator
simultaneously understands the work from multiple
angles. That breadth is genuinely valuable and
often visible to hiring managers and promotion
committees who are looking for practitioners with
well-rounded program experience.

The practitioner who can move fluidly between
strategic program oversight and tactical system-level
work is the one who adds the most value across
the full spectrum of GRC responsibilities.
