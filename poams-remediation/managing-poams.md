# Managing POAMs

![Last Reviewed](https://img.shields.io/badge/Last%20Reviewed-March%202026-green)

---

## Overview

Writing a POAM is only the beginning. The real work
of POAM management is what happens after the document
is created. A POAM that is opened and then ignored
is worse than no POAM at all because it creates a
false record of accountability without any actual
follow-through. Effective POAM management is an
ongoing, active responsibility that sits at the
center of a GRC Analyst's daily work.

---

## The GRC Analyst as POAM Manager

In most organizations the GRC Analyst is the person
responsible for maintaining the health of the POAM
inventory. This means knowing the status of every
open POAM at any given time, understanding which
ones are on track and which ones are at risk of
falling behind, communicating regularly with the
points of contact responsible for remediation, and
escalating issues when progress stalls.

A GRC Analyst who manages POAMs well is an asset
to the organization. A GRC Analyst who lets POAMs
go stale creates audit risk, authorization risk,
and reputational risk for the entire security program.

---

## Staying on Top of the POAM Inventory

The POAM inventory is a living document. It grows
as new weaknesses are identified and shrinks as
weaknesses are remediated and closed. Managing
it effectively requires a consistent routine.

| Activity | Frequency |
|----------|-----------|
| Review all open POAMs for status updates | Weekly |
| Follow up with points of contact on upcoming milestones | Weekly or biweekly |
| Update POAM status and milestone progress | As changes occur |
| Escalate stalled or delayed POAMs to leadership | As needed |
| Review upcoming scheduled completion dates | Monthly |
| Prepare POAM status reports for leadership | Monthly or as required |

---

## Communicating with System Owners and Administrators

The GRC Analyst does not remediate weaknesses
directly in most cases. That work belongs to
system administrators, engineers, and other
technical staff. The GRC Analyst's role is to
coordinate, track, and hold those stakeholders
accountable to the commitments documented in
the POAM.

This requires clear and consistent communication.
When a milestone is approaching, the GRC Analyst
reaches out to confirm that work is on track.
When a milestone is missed, the GRC Analyst
finds out why, documents the reason, and works
with the responsible party to establish a revised
plan. When a weakness is remediated, the GRC
Analyst collects and reviews the evidence before
marking the POAM closed.

The relationship between a GRC Analyst and the
system teams they support is one of the most
important professional dynamics in this work.
Being collaborative, clear, and consistent
builds trust. Being punitive or dismissive
creates friction that makes the entire program
harder to run.

---

## Handling Delays

Delays are a normal part of remediation. Systems
are complex, resources are limited, and priorities
shift. What separates a well-managed POAM from
a poorly managed one is not whether delays occur
but how they are handled when they do.

When a delay occurs the GRC Analyst should
determine the reason for the delay, document
it clearly in the POAM, establish a revised
milestone with a new realistic completion date,
and communicate the delay and revised plan to
relevant stakeholders. If a POAM is repeatedly
delayed without clear justification it may need
to be escalated to leadership for additional
attention or resources.

---

## Escalation

Not every delay can be resolved at the working
level. Sometimes remediation requires resources,
decisions, or prioritization that only leadership
can provide. A GRC Analyst needs to recognize
when a POAM has reached the point where escalation
is necessary and be prepared to communicate the
situation clearly and concisely to senior
stakeholders.

Escalation is not failure. It is a professional
judgment call that protects the organization by
ensuring that risk decisions are made at the
appropriate level of authority.

---

## Closing POAMs Correctly

A POAM is ready to be closed when the weakness
has been fully remediated and evidence of that
remediation has been collected, reviewed, and
documented. The evidence must directly support
the claim that the weakness no longer exists
or has been sufficiently mitigated.

Closing a POAM prematurely or without adequate
evidence is one of the most serious mistakes
a GRC Analyst can make. It creates a false
sense of security, misrepresents the organization's
risk posture, and will be flagged immediately
if an auditor reviews the closure.

Closed POAMs should be retained as part of the
organization's security records. They provide
historical documentation of weaknesses that
existed and the actions taken to resolve them.

---

## Key Principles

Active management matters more than perfect
documentation. A POAM that is actively tracked,
regularly updated, and honestly reported is
far more valuable than a perfectly formatted
document that no one looks at after it is created.

Communication is the foundation of effective
POAM management. The GRC Analyst who builds
strong working relationships with system owners
and administrators will always have better
visibility into POAM status than one who
manages the process purely through paperwork.

Honesty about delays is always better than
silence. Auditors and authorizing officials
expect delays. What they do not expect is
to discover delays that were never documented
or communicated.
