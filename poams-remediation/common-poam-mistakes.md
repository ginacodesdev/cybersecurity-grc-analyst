# Common POAM Mistakes

![Last Reviewed](https://img.shields.io/badge/Last%20Reviewed-March%202026-green)

---

## Overview

Even experienced GRC professionals make mistakes with
POAMs. The difference between a program that handles
them well and one that struggles is awareness. Knowing
what the most common mistakes are and why they matter
is one of the fastest ways to improve the quality
of a GRC program's documentation and audit readiness.
The mistakes covered here come up repeatedly in
assessments, audits, and authorization reviews.

---

## Mistake 1 — Vague Milestone Descriptions

A milestone that does not clearly describe what action
is being taken, who is responsible, and by when it
will be complete is not a milestone. It is a
placeholder. Vague milestones like "working on it"
or "in progress" tell an auditor nothing and signal
that the POAM is not being actively managed.

Every milestone should answer three questions. What
specific action is being taken? Who is the responsible
party? What is the target completion date?

---

## Mistake 2 — Unrealistic or Missing Completion Dates

A POAM without a scheduled completion date has no
accountability mechanism. A POAM with a completion
date set years in the future without interim milestones
is nearly as problematic. Completion dates must be
realistic, documented, and actively maintained. When
circumstances change the date should be updated with
a documented explanation not silently extended.

---

## Mistake 3 — Gaps Between Milestones

Large gaps of time between milestones where no
documented activity is occurring are one of the
most common issues identified during audits. A
milestone that spans six months or more without
interim checkpoints leaves the organization unable
to demonstrate active progress. If remediation
genuinely takes a long time, the milestones should
reflect the incremental steps happening along the
way rather than a single endpoint.

A gap of twelve months attributed to routing for
approvals is a significant red flag. Auditors will
ask why the approval process took that long and
the organization must have a documented answer.

---

## Mistake 4 — Opening POAMs and Never Updating Them

A POAM opened during an assessment and never touched
again is worse than having no POAM at all. It creates
a documented record of a known weakness with no
evidence that anyone is managing it. POAMs must be
regularly reviewed, updated, and maintained throughout
their lifecycle. Stale POAMs are one of the first
things auditors notice and one of the clearest
indicators of a program that is not functioning
as intended.

---

## Mistake 5 — Closing POAMs Without Evidence

Marking a POAM closed without collecting and documenting
evidence of remediation is a serious error. The closure
must be supported by proof that the weakness no longer
exists or has been sufficiently mitigated. Evidence
might include a vulnerability scan showing the issue
is resolved, a configuration screenshot, a signed
approval, or test results confirming the control
is functioning. Without evidence, a closed POAM
is just a claim.

---

## Mistake 6 — Misidentifying the Root Cause

Addressing the symptom of a weakness without
understanding why it occurred leads to the same
weakness reappearing. A system that falls behind
on patching needs a patching procedure, not just
a patch. A misconfigured control needs a review
of the configuration management process, not just
a one-time fix. Root cause analysis does not need
to be lengthy but it must be honest and it must
inform the remediation approach.

---

## Mistake 7 — Treating Risk Acceptance as a Default

Risk acceptance is a legitimate tool when used
deliberately and appropriately. It is not a shortcut
for avoiding difficult remediation work. When risk
acceptance POAMs become the default response to
hard problems it signals to auditors and authorizing
officials that the organization is managing its
paperwork rather than its risks. Every risk acceptance
decision should be genuinely deliberate, formally
signed off by the appropriate authority, and
periodically reviewed to determine whether
circumstances have changed.

---

## Mistake 8 — Poor Communication with Responsible Parties

A GRC Analyst who opens a POAM, assigns a point of
contact, and then does not follow up is setting the
program up for missed deadlines and audit findings.
Remediation requires coordination. The people
responsible for fixing weaknesses have competing
priorities and will not always escalate proactively
when something is delayed. The GRC Analyst must
maintain regular communication, provide reminders
as milestones approach, and follow up promptly
when deadlines are missed.

---

## Mistake 9 — Inconsistent POAM Format Across the Program

When different analysts or different systems use
different POAM formats, the organization loses the
ability to report on its overall vulnerability posture
in a consistent and meaningful way. Standardized
POAM templates, consistent terminology, and a
centralized tracking system are essential for
programs managing POAMs across multiple systems.

---

## Mistake 10 — Failing to Connect POAMs to Controls

Every POAM should be mapped to the specific security
control or requirement that the weakness relates to.
A POAM that describes a vulnerability without
connecting it to a control family or requirement
makes it difficult to understand the compliance
implications of the weakness and harder to prioritize
remediation relative to other open items.

---

## A Note on Auditor Perspective

Auditors are not looking for perfection. They are
looking for evidence that the organization knows
its risks and is managing them honestly and
accountably. A POAM inventory with well-documented
weaknesses, clear milestones, honest delay
explanations, and properly closed items tells
a story of a mature program. An inventory full
of vague entries, stale dates, and unsupported
closures tells a very different story. The goal
is always to be the organization whose POAMs
demonstrate discipline and accountability.
